🔄 Quick Refresher

Conditional Probability Recap

Remember, conditional probability means the chance of something happening given that something else already happened. The word "given" (the | symbol) shrinks your world.

P(Pass)
Out of ALL 30 students, how many passed?
20/30 = 67%
Your world = everyone
P(Pass | Studied)
Out of the 20 who studied, how many passed?
18/20 = 90%
Your world shrank to only studiers
🔄 The "given" shrinks your world. Instead of looking at everyone, you ONLY look at the group that matches the condition. That group becomes your new "total."

But Here's the Tricky Part...

P(A | B) is NOT the same as P(B | A)!

This sounds obvious, but people confuse them ALL the time. Let's see why:

P(Wet | Raining)
If it's raining, what's the chance you're wet?
≈ 95%
Almost certainly wet!
≠
P(Raining | Wet)
If you're wet, what's the chance it's raining?
≈ 30%
Could be a pool, shower, sprinkler...
⚠️ The Big Mistake: "If a sick person tests positive 99% of the time" does NOT mean "if you test positive, there's a 99% chance you're sick." These are DIFFERENT questions! Bayes' Theorem lets you flip from one to the other.
🔀 The Flip Problem — A Story

The Surprising Medical Test

Let's walk through a famous example that shocks everyone — even doctors get this wrong!

📋 The Setup
• A rare disease affects 1 in 100 people (1% of people are sick)
• A test for this disease is 90% accurate:
    — If you're sick, the test says Positive 90% of the time ✅
    — If you're healthy, the test says Negative 90% of the time ✅
    — (That means it incorrectly says Positive for healthy people 10% of the time)
❓ THE BIG QUESTION
You test POSITIVE.
What is the probability you are actually SICK?
Most people guess:
90%? 80%?
The REAL answer:
Only ~8.3%!
Wait, WHAT?! Let's see why... 👇

Let's Count It Out with 1000 People

Instead of scary formulas, let's think about 1000 real people taking the test. This makes it crystal clear!

Step 1: How many are actually sick?
1% of 1000 = 10 sick people and 990 healthy people
Step 2: Test the 10 sick people
Test is 90% accurate → 9 test Positive ✅ (correctly!) and 1 tests Negative ❌ (missed)
Step 3: Test the 990 healthy people
Test is 90% accurate for healthy people too, BUT 10% get wrong results:
10% of 990 = 99 FALSE Positives! 😱 (healthy but test says Positive)
Step 4: Count ALL Positives
Total Positive tests = 9 (truly sick) + 99 (false positives) = 108 Positive results

Of these 108 positive tests, only 9 are actually sick!
P(Sick | Positive) = 9 / 108 = 8.3%

See It Visually — 100 People

Here are 100 people. Watch what happens when we test them all:

Out of ~11 positive tests, only 1 is truly sick!
P(Sick | Positive) ≈ 1/11 ≈ 9%
🤯 Why so low? Because the disease is RARE (only 1%)! Even though the test is 90% accurate, there are SO many healthy people that even 10% false positives creates a flood of wrong results. This is called the Base Rate Fallacy — ignoring how rare the condition is.

🧮 Bayes' Formula

The Formula (Don't Panic!)

Now that you understand what's happening, here's the formula that does all those counting steps in one line:

Bayes' Theorem
P(A|B) = P(B|A) × P(A) / P(B)

That looks scary, but each piece has a friendly name:

P(A|B)
Posterior — what you WANT to know
"Given the test is positive, am I sick?" — your updated belief
P(B|A)
Likelihood — what you already know
"If someone IS sick, what's the chance they test positive?" = 90%
P(A)
Prior — your belief BEFORE seeing evidence
"What's the chance of being sick at all?" = 1% (the base rate!)
P(B)
Evidence — total probability of seeing this evidence
"What's the total chance of testing positive?" (sick + false positives)

Plugging In Our Medical Test Numbers

Known Values
P(Sick) = 0.01              ← Prior (1% have the disease) P(+test | Sick) = 0.90     ← Likelihood (90% accuracy for sick) P(+test | Healthy) = 0.10  ← False positive rate (10%)
Step 1: Calculate P(+test) — Total Evidence
P(+test) = P(+test|Sick) × P(Sick) + P(+test|Healthy) × P(Healthy) P(+test) = 0.90 × 0.01 + 0.10 × 0.99 P(+test) = 0.009 + 0.099 = 0.108
Step 2: Apply Bayes!
P(Sick | +test) = P(+test|Sick) × P(Sick) / P(+test) P(Sick | +test) = 0.90 × 0.01 / 0.108 P(Sick | +test) = 0.009 / 0.108 P(Sick | +test) = 0.083 = 8.3% ✅
# Bayes' Theorem in Python p_sick = 0.01 # Prior: 1% have disease p_pos_given_sick = 0.90 # Likelihood: test accuracy for sick p_pos_given_healthy = 0.10 # False positive rate p_healthy = 1 - p_sick p_pos = (p_pos_given_sick * p_sick) + (p_pos_given_healthy * p_healthy) # Bayes! p_sick_given_pos = (p_pos_given_sick * p_sick) / p_pos print(f"P(Sick | Positive Test) = {p_sick_given_pos:.1%}") print(f"Even with a 90% accurate test, only {p_sick_given_pos:.1%} chance!")
P(Sick | Positive Test) = 8.3% Even with a 90% accurate test, only 8.3% chance!

Think of It as Updating Your Belief

Bayes' Theorem is about updating what you believe when you get new evidence:

PRIOR
1%
Before test
↓ + Evidence (Positive Test) ↓
POSTERIOR
8.3%
After test
Your belief went from 1% → 8.3%. The evidence made it 8× more likely,
but it's still low because the disease is so rare!
🧠 Bayesian Thinking: Start with what you believe (prior). Get new evidence. Update your belief (posterior). Get MORE evidence? Update again! Each piece of evidence nudges your belief closer to the truth.

Bayesian Updating: Evidence Stacks Up!

The power of Bayes is that you can keep updating. Each new piece of evidence refines your belief. The posterior from one update becomes the prior for the next!

Start (prior)
1%
+ Test 1: Positive
8.3%
+ Test 2: Positive
45%
+ Test 3: Positive
88%
+ Symptoms match
97%
Each new evidence multiplies — from 1% → 8% → 45% → 88% → 97%. That's how doctors, detectives, and AI work — they accumulate evidence!

The Bayes' Cheat Sheet

Remember These 3 Things!
1️⃣
P(A|B) ≠ P(B|A) — "sick given positive" is NOT "positive given sick." Bayes flips between them.
2️⃣
Base rates MATTER — if something is rare, even accurate tests produce many false positives. Always ask "how common is this?"
3️⃣
Evidence updates beliefs — start with a prior, get evidence, calculate posterior. More evidence = more confident!
